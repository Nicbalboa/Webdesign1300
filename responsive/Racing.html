If you're talking about drifting in a 2017 Toyota 86, here's a few things that make it a solid choice:


Drifting Essentials

Drifting Advantages

  • Rear-Wheel Drive: Essential for drifting, the 86's RWD setup gives you the ability to break traction in the rear wheels and slide the car.
  • Low Weight and Compact Size: The car’s light weight makes it nimble and responsive, which is key when controlling a drift. It also makes it easier to throw into a drift and manage during transitions.
  • Manual Transmission: While automatic versions are available, the manual transmission option is ideal for drifting, offering more control over throttle and gear shifts.
  • Balanced Handling: The Toyota 86 is known for its nearly 50/50 weight distribution, making it very well-balanced and predictable. This is helpful when you're trying to maintain a drift for longer periods.
  • Modifications: Many drift enthusiasts modify their 86s with upgraded suspension, better tires, more power (often through turbocharging), and stronger differentials to improve their drifting performance.

Safety Requirements


If you’re thinking about taking a 2017 Toyota 86 to a drift event or track, be sure to also think about safety upgrades, like a roll cage, proper seats, harnesses, and possibly a handbrake mod to make your drifting experience more fun and secure.

Proper Equipment

  • Helmet: Always wear a certified motorsport helmet (Snell-rated or FIA-approved) to protect your head.
  • Racing Harness: Use a 5 or 6-point harness to secure yourself in the seat, reducing the risk of ejection in case of a collision or roll-over.
  • Fireproof Clothing: Wear flame-resistant suits, gloves, shoes, and socks (typically Nomex or similar material) to protect yourself in case of fire.
  • Roll Cage: Ensure your car has a roll cage if you plan to drift at a professional or competitive level. It provides protection in case of a rollover.
  • Seat and Mounting: Your racing seat should be securely mounted, and designed to withstand the forces during a drift.
